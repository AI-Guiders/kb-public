## 1. Устойчивое извлечённое знание

### A. Recasts in typical / clinical L1 (literature synthesis)

| # | Тезис | Суть |
|---|--------|------|
| **K1** | **Recast** (cross-field): immediately follows learner turn; incorporates learner elements; supplies enhanced/contrasting form while preserving basic meaning | Baker & Nelson 1984; Bohannon et al. 1996 |
| **K2** | **Corrective recasts** contingent on error; parents recast ungrammatical utterances more often than grammatical (Chouinard & Clark 2003) | Signals wrong form in conversational context |
| **K3** | **Direct Contrast** (Saxton 2005): facilitative power tied to **immediate juxtaposition** child error + adult correct model | Links to [`kb-language-acquisition-saxton-2005-recast-evidence-v1.md`](kb-language-acquisition-saxton-2005-recast-evidence-v1.md) |
| **K4** | **Nelson cognitive comparison:** acquisition from **corrective** recasts **and** non-corrective recasts that are more complex than child form (contrast without error) | Nelson 1977/1987; Bohannon et al. 1996; Nelson et al. 1996 |
| **K5** | Evidence child **notices** recast: immediate repair incorporating adult form; later spontaneous use; delayed production/comprehension on measures | Baker & Nelson 1984; Camarata et al. 1994; Chouinard & Clark 2001 |
| **K6** | **~20%** of adult reformulations precipitate immediate child repair (Clark 2014); most recasts treated as **non-corrective** (topic continuation, clarification) | Repair **not necessary** for acquisition (Nelson 2000) but supports practice/automatization when it occurs |
| **K7** | **Noticeability** depends on: (a) sequential immediacy, (b) design (explicit vs implicit), (c) developmental readiness / linguistic challenge match (Nelson 1987) | L2 «noticeability» parallel (Ellis & Sheen 2006) |
| **K8** | **Explicit** corrective recasts (L2/Sheen): declarative, short, substitution of erroneous form; prosody/pause can mark correction | Less advanced learners need more explicit recasts (Miller & Pan 2012 meta; Oliver & Mackey 2003) |
| **K9** | Recast intervention effective across SLI, autism, LD; conversational recasts may need **fewer trials** and generalize better than imitation-only (Camarata et al. 1994; Nelson et al. 1996) | Clinical tool, not CASA spec |
| **K10** | Recast efficacy in intervention **increases** with high child attention, mutual engagement, linguistically challenging but **low immediate demand** environment (Baker & Nelson 1984) | System of resources, not recast alone |

### B. AAC context (authors’ contribution)

| # | Тезис | Суть |
|---|--------|------|
| **K11** | Many AAC users show persistent expressive vocabulary / grammatical completeness deficits despite functional AAC (Binger & Light 2008; Smith 2015) | Motivation for interaction-focused intervention |
| **K12** | AAC conversations: **heavy co-construction** — adult interrogatives/expansions build child contribution incrementally; child often **confirms/denies** candidate interpretations | Asymmetry (Clarke & Wilkinson 2007; Solomon-Rice & Soto 2011) |
| **K13** | Entrenched restricted interaction patterns may **reduce** opportunities for grammaticalization in authentic conversation (Binger & Light 2008; Sutton et al. 2002) | Risk factor, not deterministic |
| **K14** | **AAC recast literature significantly limited** vs L1/L2 (Soto & Clarke in press) | Gap statement |
| **K15** | In AAC extracts, **interrogative recasts** dominate (reformulate child turn as question); children typically treat as **clarification request**, not correction — nod confirmation (Extracts 1–2) | Qualitative CA, 8 children 8–14 |
| **K16** | **Spontaneous corrective treatment** observed in subset: child repairs after declarative/interrogative recast (e.g. *want playground* → adult *You want to go to the playground?* → child *I want to go playground*) | Extracts 5–6 |
| **K17** | **Offset recasts:** adult reformulation classifiable as recast relative to earlier **platform** turn after intervening clarification Q&A (Extracts 7–8) | Violates strict «immediate next turn» definition in practice |
| **K18** | For AAC users, **immediate repair** may be **more critical** than for natural speakers: must integrate spoken input, symbol representation, device architecture, motor plans, and conversational timing | Theoretical argument; needs empirical test |
| **K19** | **Empirical evidence** for recast impact in AAC **limited**; stronger claims await convergent studies (Soto & Clarke in press) | Authors’ explicit epistemic hedge |
| **K20** | **Future design:** extend Nelson (2000) contrast paradigm (control vs recast vs model+imitation targets) to AAC; compare **spoken recasts** vs recasts on **parallel AAC device** | Research agenda, not findings |

## 2. Ограничения

- Review + qualitative illustrations; **no** controlled effect sizes for AAC recasts in this manuscript.
- Population: school-age children (8–14) with developmental disabilities using AAC — **not** typical infant L1.
- Manuscript status: no journal/DOI on consulted PDF; treat citation as **pre-publication** until verified.
- Clinical AAC recommendations **deny** direct import to CASA engineering oracle.

## 3. Связь с CASA (hypothesis only)

| Human-domain signal | CASA-relevant question (not answer) |
|---------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Interrogative vs declarative recast | Does teacher reformulation **function** as correction vs clarification in asymmetric channels? |
| Offset recast after disambiguation | Sleep/teacher timing: must contrast be **adjacent** or is clarification-then-model valid? |
| ~20% immediate repair in typical L1 | Bench metric «repair rate» may be **misleading** for acquisition |
| AAC operational load | Non-human substrate may have analogous **output cost** — when is explicit contrast worth the latency? |
