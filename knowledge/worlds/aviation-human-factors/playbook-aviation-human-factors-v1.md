# Aviation Human Factors Playbook v1

## Purpose

Operational knowledge layer for aviation psychology and human factors:
- why normal people make high-impact errors in complex cockpits,
- how teams catch (or fail to catch) those errors,
- how to design safeguards that survive stress, ambiguity, and routine pressure.

Subdomain note:
- This file is a subdomain of the aviation world root map: `playbook-aviation-v1.md`.

## Scope

- Crew Resource Management (CRM)
- Threat and Error Management (TEM)
- Aeronautical Decision Making (ADM)
- Just Culture and occurrence reporting
- Line Operations Safety Audit (LOSA)
- System-level accident causation models (Reason/HFACS style)

## Core Epistemic Contract

- Analyze events as **multi-layer system failures**, not "one bad person."
- Separate:
  - event facts,
  - likely actor intents,
  - organizational and tooling conditions,
  - consequences across time horizons.
- Mark uncertainty explicitly where evidence is incomplete.

## Canonical Operational Models

- **TEM**: threat -> error -> undesired state -> recovery.
- **CRM**: communication, authority gradient handling, workload sharing, assertiveness.
- **ADM**: perceive -> process -> perform -> evaluate loop under risk.
- **Reason model**: defense layers fail when holes align.
- **Just Culture**: protect good-faith reporting, sanction reckless/wilful violations.
- **LOSA**: observe normal ops, detect weak signals before accidents.

## Decision Rules (High Leverage)

- Any high-stakes operation must include "challenge-and-response" against single-point human misread.
- If authority gradient rises and dissent drops, treat this as a safety hazard.
- If a parameter conversion is manual and repetitive, redesign or hard-check it.
- If procedure compliance and practical reality diverge, document and resolve as system debt.

## Safety-Critical Anti-Patterns

- Punish-first response culture suppressing reporting.
- "Experienced crew means safe by default" assumption.
- Hidden mixed standards (units/procedures/phrasings) across tools and teams.
- Informal shortcuts becoming normal in marginal conditions.

## Cross-Domain Transfer Boundary

- Human-factors insights transfer well to other high-risk domains (medicine, infra ops, incident response),
  but direct policy transfer requires local regulatory and culture checks.
