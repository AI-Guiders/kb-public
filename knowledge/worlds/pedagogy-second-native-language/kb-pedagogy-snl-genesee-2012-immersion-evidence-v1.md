## Fundamentals

**Thesis:** **Immersion** = bilingual education where **≥50%** of subject-matter instruction is through the L2 in a **sheltered classroom** with continued L1 support — the canonical **second-native** school model (not classroom FL, not submersion).

**Program variants (taxonomy):**

| Dimension | Options |
|-----------|---------|
| **Start grade** | Early (K/1), delayed/middle (gr 4–5), late (gr 7+) |
| **L2 time** | Partial (~50% always), total (100% L2 for one+ grades then partial) |
| **Goals** | (a) advanced L2 functional proficiency (b) normal L1 (c) grade-level academics (d) intercultural competence |

**Definition guardrail:** minority student in L2-only class **without L1 support** = **submersion**, not immersion (Genesee & Lyster 2012).

## Outcomes — what 45+ years of research shows (Genesee 2004; G&L 2013)

### L1 (first / societal language — RU in von Neumann)

| Finding | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Long-term parity** | Immersion students match non-immersion peers on **all L1 aspects** once catch-up complete |
| **Short-term literacy lag** | During **early total immersion** (all subjects in L2), L1 **reading/writing/spelling** may lag |
| **Catch-up window** | ~**1 year** of L1 language arts → parity; e.g. L1 arts from gr 3 → parity by end gr 3 |
| **Delayed/late start** | Middle/late immersion: **no** typical L1 literacy lags (L1 already developed) |
| **Exposure paradox** | More/earlier **L1 instructional time** does **not** reliably beat less L1 time for final L1 achievement |

### L2 (second-native — EN)

| Finding | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| vs **traditional FL** | **Significantly superior** functional proficiency (all skills; comprehension often > production) |
| vs **native speakers** | After 10–11 years: **not** native-like expressive grammar (lexical/syntactic L1 transfer, restricted vocabulary, non-idiomatic usage) |
| Harley et al. (1990), age 11–12 | = natives on **discourse** competence; **below** natives on **grammar** (especially verbs) and **all sociolinguistic** measures |
| **More L2 exposure** | Generally → higher L2 proficiency (total > partial immersion) |
| **Suitability** | Wide range: SES, ability levels, typologically similar/dissimilar L1–L2 pairs, majority and minority students |

### Academic content

- **Parity** with L1-instruction peers in math, science, social studies when assessed in **appropriate language** and L2 proficiency accounted for.
- Content-through-L2 does **not** impede acquiring new academic knowledge vs L1-medium instruction.

## Pedagogy — content alone is not enough

**Initial immersion theory:** learn L2 through **meaningful content** (focus on meaning, Snow–Met–Genesee rationale).

**Documented gap:** exclusive meaning-focus → students **plateau** on grammar, lexicon, sociolinguistic competence; can «get by» with limited interlanguage (Genesee 1987; Swain 1985, 1998).

**Recommended: counterbalanced immersion** (Genesee & Lyster 2012):

| Layer | Practice |
|-------|----------|
| **Input** | Comprehension facilitation **and** noticing/salience activities |
| **Production** | Content tasks **and** proceduralization of avoided forms |
| **Interaction** | Implicit feedback (recasts) → prompts pushing beyond interlanguage |
| **Form-focused instruction** | Embedded in meaningful tasks — **≠** decontextualized grammar (verb paradigms) |
| **Vocabulary** | Beyond comprehension — generative/morphological awareness |
| **Grammar** | Form–meaning mappings in content context; Norris & Ortega (2000) meta: explicit form-focus **>** implicit |

**Lyster (2004b) synthesis:** form-oriented treatments **>** meaning-only content tasks for recalcitrant forms (Harley aspect; Day & Shapson conditionals; Lyster tu/vous, gender).

**Link to Long FoF card:** immersion needs **Focus on Form**, not **Focus on Forms** syllabus.

## Age & time (Genesee 2004 — school context)

| Topic | Evidence summary |
|-------|------------------|
| **Early start** | Higher ultimate L2 proficiency given **equal total exposure**; EFI > MFI > LFI overall (Wesche et al. 1996) |
| **Late start** | Can reach **near-early** L2 levels by end secondary with less total hours (Genesee 1981 Montreal); older learners effective when motivated + L1 literacy developed |
| **No universal critical period** for school L2; community goals/resources determine start grade |
| **Time on task** | Important but **not linear**; 80% vs 40% late-immersion day **did not** consistently favor 80% (Stevens 1983); intensity matters |
| **Lower bound** | 20 vs 30 min/day FL-style exposure ≈ meaningless difference |

**von Neumann implication:** early EN oral/content immersion **if** long secondary continuation resourced; late start viable with strong RU literacy + prior EN exposure — not an excuse for minimal hours.

## At-risk learners & L2 literacy

- Immersion **generally** suitable for diverse ability; **reading difficulty** drives attrition (Obadia & Thériault 1997).
- Early identification + targeted intervention (as in monolingual RTI) recommended (Genesee 2007 strand).

| K1 | Second-native minimum: **≥50% curriculum in L2** + **sheltered L1 support** — not FL electives |
| K2 | Expect **temporary L1 literacy lag** in early total immersion; plan **L1 arts year** explicitly |
| K3 | L2 oral/comprehension can look strong while **grammar/sociolinguistic** competence non-native — plan FoF counterbalance |
| K4 | **Deny** native-like L2 grammar as short-term gate; **require** form-rich pedagogy for long-term growth |
